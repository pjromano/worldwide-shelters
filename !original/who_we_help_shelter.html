  Health Shelter Link
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
“The quality of shelter available has a great impact on health and well-being.”
— WHO Shelter and Emergency Settlements Handbook, Vol. II

Providing high-quality shelter at cost helps prevent major health issues from occurring in the first place because many more people have the benefit of higher living standards. In addition, our shelters are designed to keep living and sleeping space free of harmful elements. WWS standards include screens and shelters impregnated with long-lasting insecticide to reduce the risk of transmitted diseases. In addition, the shelters themselves can be used to prevent the spread of disease by making it easier to isolate those who are sick.

As stated in 2009 Sphere Standards, Humanitarian Charter and Minimum Standards in Disaster Response:

“Shelter is a critical determinant for survival in the initial stages of a disaster. Beyond survival, shelter is necessary to provide security and personal safety, protection from the climate and enhanced resistance to ill health and disease.”

“Vector control measures that are incorporated into the design and materials are selected to minimize health hazards.”

A vector is a disease-carrying agent and the sad fact is, in many disaster situations, vector-borne diseases are a major cause of sickness and death. Vector examples include mosquitoes, which are responsible for the transmission of malaria, yellow fever and dengue haemorrhagic fever. The non-biting house fly, blow fly and flesh fly are key agents for the transmission of diarrhoeal disease. Biting flies, bed bugs and fleas transmit murine typhus and plague. Ticks and human body lice transmit typhus and relapsing fever. Rats and mice transmit diseases such as leptospirosis and Lassa fever and are hosts for other vectors like fleas. Vector-borne diseases can be controlled through a variety of initiatives, and appropriate shelter is key among them.

Displacement causes a dramatic disruption to an individual’s life. For IDPs, poor shelter conditions, combined with the lack of medical and sanitation services and insufficient food, result in significantly higher disease levels. In many situations, IDP living conditions are not very different from those of the non-displaced communities around them. This situation was discovered in 2008 in Burundi, Central African Republic, Somalia and Zimbabwe.

In addition, the influx of IDPs puts existing health care services under severe strain, reducing access to care for both displaced and local populations. This was the case in 2008 in Côte d’Ivoire and Sri Lanka. IDPs in urban centers from Afghanistan to Colombia face the same conditions as other poor urban residents.

The need for adequate shelter among the world’s children underscores the important role shelter plays in supporting health and education and eliminating poverty. Yet over 640 million children in developing countries experience severe shelter deprivation with those in sub-Saharan Africa the most deprived. Lack of adequate shelter is also widespread in South Asia, the Middle East and North Africa where rural children are four times more likely than urban dwellers to be shelter-deprived.

UNICEF’s The State of the World’s Children report puts shelter as the number one deprivation among children in the developing world. Adequate shelter is a primary right for children and in four of the six regions of the world, they are severely deprived of this right.

Finally, Worldwide Shelters is firmly committed to the UN Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) to prevent and eliminate disease and poverty and to support education, environmental sustainability and human rights. Four of the eight MDG goals address issues of health, hunger and poverty. Providing quality human shelter directly supports these goals.

As hunger relates to shelter, the less time spent on fixing inadequate shelter, the more time that can be spent on supplying some of the other basic needs — food, water, clothing, employment, and medical care.
